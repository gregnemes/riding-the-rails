Train stations are not open to the general public. Entry is highly secured, requiring a national ID or passport, a ticket, and a wanded security scan upon arrival. Source: Author.
Although Chinese stations are large and all have several access points, there are usually long queues at the entrance. Source: Author.
In some stations, access is blocked by ad-hoc locking mechanisms so all passengers are filtered through security. Source: Author.
Bags are scanned and humans are wanded in the vestibule. Source: Author.
Once inside, a bank of multilevel escalators takes passengers up to a vast waiting area that hovers above the platforms and railway corridor. Source: Author.
A few shops and caf√©s line the edge of this enormous space, sometimes separated on a mezzanine level. Source: Author.
The trains arrive and depart via platforms below the waiting area. Access to the platforms below is granted only a few minutes before the train departure, so a long queue forms again before access to the platform is granted. Tickets are checked again via turnstile swipe.
The queue is frenetic. Even though all tickets are assigned by seat, there is a sense of urgency among the passengers. Pushing and yelling are a common occurrence. Source: Author.
When the train arrives, passengers crowd the doors and push through quickly to their seats. A conductor checks tickets a third time after the train has departed. Source: Author.
Upon arrival at the destination, passengers disembark the train onto the platform and are escorted down a flight of stairs by security guards. Tickets are checked a fourth and final time via a turnstile swipe to exit. Source: Author.

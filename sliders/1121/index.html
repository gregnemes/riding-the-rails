Watchman's shanties are small structures for housing watchmen along railroads at crossings, drawbridges, sharp curves, and other dangerous points. This shanty near Milliken University was in operation until 1910.
Watchman's shanties are among the first "prefab" structures in the US, because the designs for the shanties are often standardized along a railroad line. Left: Standard Shanty, Richmond and Alleghany Railroad. Right: Standard Philadelphia & Reading Railroad Shanty. Source: W. Berg.
Left: Standard Shanty of Narrow Width, Pennsylvania Railroad. Right: Standard Shanty, Norfolk & Western Railroad. Source: W. Berg.
Section tool houses or handcar houses are used for storing handcars, tools, and supplies required for track construction and maintenance. They also provided shelter to workmen during very heavy or prolonged storms. Source: W. Berg.
Snowsheds, both freestanding and built into mountainsides, were important features on northern railroads. Source: W. Berg.
Signal towers are used on railroads where it is necessary to station a signalman above the railroad to provide a view of the tracks and surroundings, or to allow the signalman to be readily seen by approaching trains. Source: W. Berg.
Car sheds are provided on railroads to protect expensive passenger or private cars from the weather and to allow the cars to be cleaned under cover. Source: W. Berg.
Ash-pits or clinker-pits are required along the main line of a railroad and at yards to allow ashes and clinkers collecting in the fireboxes of engines to be dumped. Source: W. Berg.
Sand was required to increase traction for the driving wheels of steam trains. A special box in the locomotive distributed sand directly on the tracks. Source: W. Berg.
Sand houses stored and dried sand wherever engines were supplied with coal, water, oil, etc., before starting on a run. Source: W. Berg.
Oil mixing houses were constructed for the purpose of mixing oils for illuminating, signaling, and lubricating purposes. Source: W. Berg.
Oil mixing houses were generally found at large stations and transfer points. Elsewhere, pre-mixed oils were used and stored in oil storage houses. Source: W. Berg.
Oil storage houses hold and preserve oils used to lubricate (engines, cars, and shop machinery) and to illuminate (engine headlights, signal and switch lamps, and lamps in cars, station buildings, and station grounds). Source: W. Berg.
Water stations were key for steam locomotives. Depending on route traffic, a water tower was located every five to twenty miles.
Tenders (coal cars located directly behind the locomotive) are filled with coal from above by special coaling facilities. The coal is heavy, but must be handled with care to avoid breakage. Source: W. Berg.
Engine houses provide facilities for storing, cleaning, and making repairs to locomotives. Circular houses have turntables for ease of access. Source: W. Berg.

What is High Speed Rail

High speed rail (HSR) is a further development of conventional rail technologies that allows for very high speeds using electricity for power. It is a mobility system that involves not simply specialized rolling stock (train cars), but also improvements to railway infrastructure, including civil engineering features such as tunnels and viaducts; the allocation of a robust electrical power source and catenary distribution system; and miles of rails, tracks, and ballast along the route.

What is High Speed Rail

High speed rail will never replace airplanes or automobiles. For short trips, the time required to travel from your home to a train station before departure, and from the station to your final destination upon arrival, often add enough time to the overall journey that a car makes more sense than a train. For longer trips, the faster speeds of an airplane make more sense than a train. However, for distances between about 100 to 500 miles, HSR is often the fastest and most convenient mode of transit. This range of distances, generally known as the ‘HSR Sweet Spot’, will only increase as air travel becomes more crowded with longer delays.

What is High Speed Rail

The optimal operation scenario for high speed rail is a closed system: tracks designed specifically for, and dedicated to, high speed train use. These routes are disconnected from local service, and grade separated from automobile traffic. Currently, this idealized scenario only exists in Japan. In most cases, dedicated high speed tracks make up only a portion of the route and high speed trains share tracks with slower commuter services on the approach to a city station, where space for new tracks is limited. This track sharing operation method is called ‘blended service’ and usually has an adverse effect on operation of high speed services. Still, many important HSR routes use blended service, including the highly successful Paris-Lyon route and Frankfurt-Cologne route. Another example of blended service exists in Germany, where high speed passenger service operates in the day, and freight service operates on the same routes overnight. (This arrangement presents its own challenges, due to the high maintenance demands of high speed rail tracks and the need to service them after normal passenger service is complete for the day.) Still another example exists in the UK, where high speed commuter trains share service on the HSR route to the Chunnel.

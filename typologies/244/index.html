1 / 3: Ashford International Station

Ashford is a suburb in the county of Kent, about sixty-one miles southeast of central London. Along with Ebbsfleet and Folkestone, Ashford received an international HSR station as a part of the HS1 high-speed rail development from London to the European continent. This new international station is located across the expanded track corridor from the existing regional station.

Since the station opened in 2009, Ashford now has direct access to destinations such as Paris Gare du Nord, Brussels Midi, Disneyland Paris, Marseilles, and Bourg-Saint-Maurice. Like the Massy TGV station, short trips en route to London on International Eurostar trains are not permitted. (Stops at the Ashford station by high-speed trains headed to London are for drop-off only, and no passengers are allowed to board these trains.) But unlike the situation at the Massy TGV station, a higher-speed commuter train service is in operation on the HS1 route through London's southeastern suburbs. These "Javelin" trains dramatically decrease daily commuting times: the travel time from Ashford to London before the opening of HS1 was 1 hour and 24 minutes. After, the fastest journey is just 37 minutes. Moreover, the Javelin services have relieved capacity on the existing regional lines, such that conventional commuter trains can run more frequently and with greater reliability now that international trains are routed on the high-speed lines.

The development of high-speed rail should serve local suburbs as well as regional passengers, and this case study provides an excellent example of how this can be achieved.

2 / 3: Shanghai South Station

To accommodate southbound high-speed rail lines to Hangzhou and other cities in Zhejiang province (and slower regional routes), Shanghai South Station was opened in 2006 as the first "circular" train station in the world. (Beijing adopted a circular design for it’s own South Station soon after.) True to its name, the station is located south of central Shanghai in a suburban* neighborhood.

Like most Chinese HSR stations, South Station is huge: almost 900 feet in diameter and 150 feet above grade, with additional below-grade development. Unlike most Chinese stations, however, is the nuanced integration of South Station into the neighborhood fabric. Instead of being encircled with the typical layers of vast public plazas and multi-lane highways, South Station has a distinctly suburban disposition. The main structure is surrounded by a verdant sunken lawn with pedestrian access at both the lower levels and via bridges that span over to the adjacent neighborhoods at ground level. The highway is accessed by ramps that circle the station, a circulation path that is separated from local traffic. A large, subterranean shopping mall is connected to the station via the bridges and sunken lawn, leaving the grounds around the station free of buildings and more park-like in character. The density of uses, variety of programs, and high quantity of passengers through the station have, in the short time since construction, established this station as a hub for the community.

Despite its suburban* location, Shanghai South Station is successful in creating a sense of community in the neighborhood and providing access to shops, restaurants, and park space for local residents.

*Suburban by Chinese standards.

3 / 3: Gare Aix-en-Provence

Similar to Avignon, the Aix-en-Provence region is celebrated for its weather, the beauty of the natural environment, and it's historic city centers. The dense urban core of Aix was deemed too challenging and expensive for the location of a TGV station; instead, a suburban site was chosen. In this case, the siting is much further from the city than in the case of Avignon: the Aix TGV station is a full ten miles southwest of the city in a remote area between several smaller communities.

The Aix-en-Provence TGV Station is actually situated above the intersection of a major highway and the high-speed rail route from Lyon to Marseilles: the lowest level is the highway, the second level (above the highway) is the high-speed rail, and above is the station, along with a large quantity of parking (operated by private companies) and a generous drop-off/pick-up route connected to local roads. Pedestrian connection to the surrounding area is limited, but as the location is so remote, there isn’t really anywhere to go on foot anyway and illegally parked cars block the few narrow sidewalks that exist. Most passengers access the station via automobile.

It’s hard to imagine, given the architectural solution and remote location, that the Aix TGV station will ever catalyze significant new development around the station or provide significant benefit to nearby residents besides TGV access. Indeed, in the fifteen years the station has been in operation, no significant peripheral development has occurred.
